Unit 1: The Renaissance

A "rebirth" of classical art, learning, and human-centered thinking that marked the transition from the Middle Ages to the modern world.

Why Italy? The Causes

  • Trade & Wealth: Strategic Mediterranean location made city-states like Florence & Venice rich.
  • Classical Heritage: Surrounded by the ruins and legacy of the Roman Empire.
  • Patronage: Wealthy families like the Medici funded art and scholarship.
  • New Ideas: Influx of classical texts and scholars from the Byzantine Empire.

A New Vision: Humanism

A shift from medieval religious focus to human potential, achievement, and reason. Celebrated the "Renaissance Man" ideal – a person skilled in many fields.

Triangular Trade & its Consequences

A brutal system of trade linking Europe, Africa, and the Americas. It fueled colonial economies but resulted in the horrific Atlantic Slave Trade, forcibly displacing millions of Africans and causing immeasurable suffering. The wealth it generated for Europe was built on the foundation of exploitation.

Treaty of Tordesillas (1494)

A papal decree that divided the newly "discovered" world between Spain (west of the line) and Portugal (east of the line), profoundly shaping the colonial map of South America.

Unit 3 & 4: An Age of Revolutions

The American and French Revolutions shook the world, overthrowing old orders and introducing powerful new ideas about rights, liberty, and governance.

The American Revolution (c. 1763-1783)

A struggle for independence based on the principle of "No taxation without representation," leading to the birth of the United States.

The French Revolution (c. 1789-1815)

A radical upheaval to dismantle the Ancien Régime under the banner of "Liberty, Equality, Fraternity," which led to a republic, terror, and the rise of Napoleon.

The Three Estates: A visualization of the deep social inequality in pre-revolutionary France.

The Quebec Connection: A Tale of Two Revolutions

The outcomes of these two revolutions profoundly shaped Quebec's destiny. The American Revolution led to the influx of United Empire Loyalists, which dramatically increased the English-speaking population and led directly to the Constitutional Act of 1791. This act divided Quebec into Upper Canada (Ontario) and Lower Canada (Quebec), entrenching Canada's bicultural reality. Conversely, the French Revolution's anti-clerical violence horrified the Catholic society of Lower Canada, reinforcing conservatism and loyalty to the British Crown as a shield against "godless" republicanism.

Unit 5: The Industrial Revolution

A monumental shift from agrarian life to an economy based on factories, machines, and new power sources, creating immense wealth and immense challenges.

Why Britain First?

A unique combination of factors made Britain the birthplace of industrialization.

  • Agricultural Revolution
  • Abundant Coal & Iron
  • Capital for Investment
  • Colonial Markets
  • Political Stability
  • Transportation Network

Societal Transformation

The Rise of the City

The factory system drew millions from the countryside to rapidly growing, often overcrowded and unsanitary, urban centers. This chart shows the dramatic population boom in Manchester, a key industrial city.

New Social Classes

  • Industrial Bourgeoisie The new wealthy class of factory owners, inventors, and capitalists who amassed great fortunes.
  • The Proletariat (Working Class) The large class of urban wage-earners who sold their labor to survive. They faced long hours, low pay, dangerous conditions, and lived in poor housing.

Responses to Industrialization

In response to harsh conditions, workers began to organize into labor unions to demand better wages, hours, and conditions. New political ideas like socialism and communism also emerged, challenging the capitalist system.
