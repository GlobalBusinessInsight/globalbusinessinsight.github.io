Ukraine's Economic Lifeline: Balancing on the Brink

If Russia is reorganizing internal resources, Ukraine is surviving on external life support and extreme mobilization.

If Russia's war economy is a sealed machine sustaining itself through internal reorganization, Ukraine's is a complex organism kept alive by external transfusions and extreme internal mobilization. Entering the fourth year of full-scale war, Kyiv faces a nearly impossible fiscal trilemma: funding a massive army, maintaining a battered society, and preventing total financial collapse.

The answer lies in a "hybrid survival model": brutal centralization of domestic taxes, a monetary tightrope walk, and international aid as an absolute pillar.

1. Domestic Taxation: From "Leniency" to "Maximum Squeeze"

In the early days of 2022, to prevent economic shock, the Ukrainian government implemented radically loose tax policies (such as the 2% turnover tax). But by 2024 and 2025, such leniency was history. Facing a gaping budget deficit, Kyiv was forced to pivot to "fiscal radicalism."

The most significant change is the sharp increase in the Military Levy. New legislation effective in early 2025 hiked the military tax on personal income from 1.5% to 5% and expanded it to include previously exempt sole proprietors (FOPs). This means every Ukrainian worker and small shopkeeper is now directly paying for the front line.

Furthermore, the government targeted sectors profiting from the war economy. Banks were hit with a retrospective 50% excess profits tax (dropping to 25% from 2024), a move that, while angering the financial sector, brought desperately needed cash to the treasury. Crucially, almost every cent of domestic tax revenue goes exclusively to defense. It is a brutal accounting reality: what Ukraine earns buys bullets; everything else (salaries, pensions, healthcare) relies on allies.

2. Regional Finance: The Nationalization of the "Military Dividend"

Unlike Russian regions which serve as shock absorbers, Ukraine's local finances experienced a dramatic "rollercoaster" followed by "nationalization."

In 2022-2023, communities hosting military units became unexpectedly wealthy. Under Ukrainian law, a portion of the Personal Income Tax (PIT) from soldiers' salaries stayed local. As the army expanded and combat pay increased, this so-called "Military PIT" became a massive windfall for local governments.

However, this imbalance alarmed the center. In late 2023, the Verkhovna Rada (Parliament) passed a highly controversial decision: to strip the "Military PIT" from local budgets and redirect it entirely to the central budget, specifically to the State Special Transport Service and the Ministry of Strategic Industries for domestic weapon production (drones and missiles). This redistribution (approx. 96 billion UAH) marked an absolute centralization of fiscal power—money for local roads became money for national artillery.

3. Monetary Policy: Walking the Wire over Ruins

The National Bank of Ukraine (NBU), led by Andriy Pyshny, has executed a textbook case of crisis management.

Unlike Russia, which leans on petrodollars to prop up the ruble, Ukraine must defend the hryvnia under the crushing weight of a massive trade deficit (a record $39.9 billion deficit in goods and services for the first 9 months of 2025). The central bank abandoned the early-war fixed peg for "managed flexibility," allowing the exchange rate to fluctuate moderately to absorb shocks while still intervening to prevent panic.

To curb inflation (bouncing back to around 10% in 2024-2025), the NBU has kept the key policy rate high at 15.5%. While this stifles business lending and recovery, it has successfully preserved the value of Ukrainian savings, avoiding the hyperinflationary spirals of the 1990s. It is a painful but necessary trade-off: sacrificing growth for stability.

4. International Aid: From "Transfusion" to "Dialysis"

This is the fundamental difference between the Ukrainian and Russian economies. Foreign aid is not a supplement; it is the foundation. In 2024, international aid covered approximately 66% of the budget deficit. Without this money, Ukraine would be bankrupt within weeks.

However, the nature of this aid is shifting profoundly. In 2022-2023, much of it was grants (free money). By 2024-2025, aid has increasingly formalized into loans. The critical pivot is the G7's "ERA mechanism" (Extraordinary Revenue Acceleration)—a $50 billion loan package to Ukraine, repaid not by Ukrainian taxpayers, but by the interest generated from frozen Russian sovereign assets.

This means Ukraine's fiscal lifeline is moving from "Western goodwill" to "Russian assets." While this reduces the direct burden on Western taxpayers, it adds complexity and uncertainty to the financing structure.

5. The Outlook: An Endurance Race on the Edge

Ukraine's economic potential is being squeezed by both physical destruction and a demographic crisis. Russian bombardment of energy infrastructure limits industrial output, while mobilization and emigration have caused severe labor shortages. GDP growth forecasts for 2025 have been downgraded to a meager 1.9%.

Ukraine's ability to sustain the war no longer depends on traditional economic indicators, but on two variables: the political will of Western aid (will it arrive on time?) and the breaking point of domestic society (can they endure 5% military taxes and high inflation?).

If Russia is burning its future wealth to win today, Ukraine is leveraging its future sovereign credit (through massive debt) to survive today. It is a brutal race of endurance: will Russia's oil reserves run dry before the West's financial pipelines run empty?

财经与经济

乌克兰的经济生命线：生存边缘的财政平衡术

如果说俄罗斯是在进行内部资源重组，那么乌克兰则是在依靠外部输血和极限动员来维持生存。

如果说俄罗斯的战时经济是一台通过内部重组来自我维持的封闭机器，那么乌克兰的战时经济则像是一个依靠外部输血和内部极限动员维持生命的复杂有机体。进入全面战争的第四个年头，基辅面临着一个几乎不可能完成的财政三难困境：为庞大的军队提供资金、维持遭到重创的社会运转，以及防止金融体系彻底崩溃。

答案在于一种“混合生存模式”：国内税收的残酷集中化、货币政策的走钢丝表演，以及作为绝对支柱的国际援助。

1. 中央税收：从“宽容”到“极限压榨”

战争初期的2022年，为了防止经济休克，乌克兰政府曾实施了极度宽松的税收政策（如允许企业只缴纳2%的营业额税）。但到了2024年和2025年，这种宽容已成为历史。面对巨大的预算赤字，基辅被迫转向“财政激进主义”。

最显著的变化是军事税（Military Levy）的急剧增加。2025年初生效的新立法将个人收入的军事税率从1.5%直接拉升至5%，并将征收范围扩大到以前免税的个体经营者（FOPs）。这意味着每一位乌克兰工人和小商贩都在直接为前线买单。

此外，政府将目光投向了在战争中获利的特定行业。银行被征收了追溯性的50%超额利润税（2024年起降为25%），这一举措虽然激怒了银行业，但为国库带来了急需的现金。所有的这些国内税收——每一分钱——几乎都完全用于国防。这是一个残酷的会计现实：乌克兰自己赚的钱只够买子弹，其他一切（工资、养老金、医疗）都要靠盟友。

2. 地方财政：被收归国有的“军事红利”

与俄罗斯各地区被迫承担额外支出不同，乌克兰的地方财政经历了一场戏剧性的“过山车”和随后的“国有化”。

在2022-2023年，许多驻扎部队的地区意外变得富裕。根据乌克兰法律，士兵工资中的个人所得税（PIT）部分归地方所有。随着军队规模扩大和战斗津贴增加，所谓的“军事PIT”成为了地方政府的巨额意外之财。

然而，这种分配上的不平衡引起了中央政府的警觉。2023年底，最高拉达（议会）通过了一项争议巨大的决定：将“军事PIT”从地方预算中剥离，全部上缴中央，直接划拨给国家特种运输局和战略产业部，用于国内武器生产（如无人机和导弹）。这笔资金规模巨大（约960亿格里夫纳），它的重新分配标志着财政权力的绝对集中——为了胜利，地方的修路钱变成了中央的造炮钱。

3. 货币政策：在废墟上走钢丝

乌克兰国家银行（NBU）在行长安德烈·皮什尼（Andriy Pyshny）的带领下，执行了一场教科书级的危机管理。

不同于俄罗斯依靠石油美元支撑卢布，乌克兰必须在巨额贸易逆差（2025年前9个月商品和服务贸易逆差达到创纪录的399亿美元）的压力下捍卫格里夫纳。央行放弃了战争初期的固定汇率，转向“有管理的灵活性”，允许汇率适度波动以吸收冲击，但仍动用外汇储备进行干预以防止恐慌。

为了遏制通胀（2024-2025年反弹至10%左右），央行将基准利率维持在15.5%的高位。这虽然抑制了商业贷款和经济复苏，但成功保住了乌克兰人的储蓄价值，避免了像90年代那样的恶性通货膨胀。这是一个痛苦但必要的权衡：牺牲增长以换取稳定。

4. 国际援助：从“输血”到“透析”

这是乌克兰经济与俄罗斯最根本的区别。外来援助不是补充，而是支柱。在2024年，国际援助覆盖了大约66%的预算赤字。没有这笔钱，乌克兰将在数周内破产。

然而，援助的性质正在发生深刻变化。2022-2023年，大量援助是赠款（无需偿还）。到了2024-2025年，援助越来越形式化为贷款。最为关键的转折是G7达成的“ERA机制”（Extraordinary Revenue Acceleration），即向乌克兰提供500亿美元贷款，但偿还资金来源并非乌克兰纳税人，而是被冻结的俄罗斯主权资产产生的利息收益。

这意味着乌克兰的财政生命线正在从“西方的善意”转向“俄罗斯的资产”。这虽然降低了西方纳税人的直接负担，但也增加了融资结构的复杂性和不确定性。

5. 展望：在深渊边缘的耐力赛

乌克兰的经济潜力正在被物理破坏和人口危机双重挤压。俄罗斯对能源基础设施的持续轰炸限制了工业产出，而动员和移民导致了严重的劳动力短缺。2025年的GDP增长预测已被下调至微不足道的1.9%。

乌克兰支撑战争的能力不再取决于传统的经济指标，而取决于两个变量：西方援助的政治意愿（能否持续到位）和国内社会的承受极限（能否忍受5%的军事税和高通胀）。

如果说俄罗斯是在燃烧未来的财富来换取现在的胜利，那么乌克兰则是在透支未来的主权信用（通过巨额债务）来换取现在的生存。这就形成了一场残酷的耐力赛：是俄罗斯的石油储备先耗尽，还是西方的资金管道先枯竭？
